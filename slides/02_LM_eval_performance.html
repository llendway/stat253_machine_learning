A Familiar Statistic: \(R^2\)

\[ R^2 = 1 - \frac{RSS}{SST} = 1 - \frac{\sum(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum(y_i - \bar{y})^2} \]




\(R^2\) is the proportion of variation in the response variable explained by the model.



One way we can measure variation in a variable is the total sum of squares, or SST for short. It is calculated as the sum of the squared differences between the actual response values, the \(y_i\)s and the mean response value, \(\bar{y}\). These differences are indicated for a few observations in the graph on the left in blue. The SST is about 25,922.

The second plot indicates some residuals in red. Summing all the squared residuals gives us the residual sum of squares, RSS. This tells us the amount of variation still left in the response variable after accounting for the explanatory variable.

So, RSS divided by SST is the proportion of variation in the response that is left unexplained, which means 1 - RSS/SST is the proportion of variation in the response explained by the model. That is the definition of \(R^2\). For this model, the \(R^2\) is about 0.525.

\(R^2\) is always between 0 and 1. Values closer to 1 indicate a stronger model. So, let’s use \(R^2\) to evaluate model performance … right?

Well, as it turns out, \(R^2\) is not the greatest statistic to use to measure model performance. In the model shown here, I have added 2 “garbage” variables to the model. By garbage, I mean that each variable contains random numbers from a standard normal distribution. You can think of them as noise variables.

From the model output, we can see that, after accounting for all other variables in the model, none of the garbage variables is statistically significant. But, we also see that the \(R^2\) has increased from the model with no garbage variables. And this will always be the case: \(R^2\) will always increase with the addition of more explanatory variables, even if they are completely useless.

Adj. \(R^2\)

If we have \(n\) observations and \(p\) is the number of explanatory variables in the model,

\[ Adj. R^2 = 1 - \Big[(1 - R^2)\frac{n-1}{n-p-1}\Big] \]

As the number of predictors, \(p\), increases, \(R^2\) increases, which means \(1-R^2\) decreases. But also as \(p\) increases \(\frac{n-1}{n-p-1}\) increases.

Adjusted \(R^2\) is an improvment of the \(R^2\) statistic. If we have \(n\) observations and \(p\) is the number of explanatory variables in the model, the formula for adjusted \(R^2\) is defined as in the equation at the top.

When there are no predictors, this is exactly equal to \(R^2\). Otherwise, it will be less than \(R^2\) (you can take time on your own to prove that mathematically, if you’d like).

The part inside square brackets on the right side of the equation has two competing pieces. Where the \(1-R^2\) piece decreases as the number of predictors increases, the fraction \(\frac{n-1}{n-p-1}\) increases. In order for the entire piece inside square brackets to decrease (thus increasing adjusted \(R^2\)), the \(R^2\) has to increase a large enough amount to overpower the \(\frac{n-1}{n-p-1}\) fraction.

Adj. \(R^2\) example

In the two models we examined, we see that even though \(R^2\) increases, adjusted \(R^2\) decreases when the garbage variables are added to the model.













model r.squared adj.r.squared
Only Displacement 0.5248 0.5242
Displacement and garbage 0.5254 0.5236

In the two models we examined, we see that even though \(R^2\) increases, adjusted \(R^2\) decreases when the garbage variables are added to the model.

Although this statistic is better than ordinary \(R^2\), both of these are dependent on the variation in the response. It can be easier to explain a larger proportion of the variation in response variables that are highly variable. And, even if a lot of variation is explained, the results may not be very accurate, meaning predictions could still be expected to be quite far off from their actual values. That leads us to our next statistic - root mean squared error.

RMSE

The root mean squared error, RMSE, is computed as

\[ RMSE = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}, \]

and is the square root of the average sum of the squared residuals. In practice, this quantity tells us approximately the average distance between the actual and predicted values. Or, on average, how far off a prediction will be.

The RMSE for the model that only uses Displacement to model MPG is computed below.

augment(cars_displ) %>% 
  summarize(rmse = sqrt(mean(.resid^2)))
## # A tibble: 1 x 1
##    rmse
##   <dbl>
## 1  3.92

The formula for the root mean squared error, RMSE for short, is shown here. It is the square root of the average sum of the squared residuals. The RMSE means that, on average, the predicted MPG will be about 3.92 MPG off the actual MPG. So, it seems like we have a good measure of model performance or accuracy now … right?
